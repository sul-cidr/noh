Form

Zeami (c.1363- c.1443) is credited with having perfected Noh as it exists today, thus most formal analyses of Noh plays refer to his adaptation of the Gagaku concept of modulation and movement called Jo-ha-kyū. The tripartite can be summarized as: Jo - slow, Ha - development / acceleration, and kyū - fast conclusion, suggesting that most efforts should start slowly, speed up, before rapidly ending. Zeami went on and expanded the model from three to five parts by incorporating the Jo-ha-kyū concept into the Ha, the tripartite’s longest section. Thus, his adaptation can be summarized as: Jo, Ha-jo, Ha-ha, Ha-kyū, Kyū.

Eventually, the principle of Jo-ha-kyū was applied to various levels of organization. For instance, its basic idea of acceleration has shaped declamation and movements, among others. Moreover, during the Edo period (1603-1868) plays ended up being classified into five categories, each one determined first and foremost by the type of shite but also by elements such as the type of dance performed in the play, the subject matter, and the dramatic construction, among others:

Category 1 (± 16% of the repertoire): The shite is often a deity and these plays have a religious and ceremonial undertone.
Category 2 (± 7% of the repertoire): The shite is often the ghost of a warrior who died in battle.
Category 3 (± 17% of the repertoire): The shite is usually a young and beautiful woman.
Category 4 (± 38% of the repertoire): The shite is a mad or revengeful character, among several other options.
Category 5 (± 22% of the repertoire): The shite is often a dragon or demon.

Consequently, a full Noh performance used to include five plays, one of each categories, performed in the order corresponding to its category's number.

Although there are few one-act plays, Zeami’s contribution includes defining the basic formal structure of the two-act play. Based on his five-part adaptation of the Jo-ha-kyū concept, he proposed two acts, each one divided up into five parts (dan), according to the following sequence of actions:
First act (mae ba): Waki enters, Shite enters, Dialogue, Shite performs, and Shite exits
Second act (nochi ba): Waki waits, Shite re-enters, Dialogue, Shite performs, and Shite exits

A nakairi is often performed between the two acts, yet there are no shōdan associated with it. Usually, an ai-kyōgen enters at that moment and engages with the waki. Acting as a local who is well informed about the place’s history, he introduces information that connects the first and second acts.

Shōdan

The music of Noh can be seen as modular not only because of the prescribed vocabulary of melodic and rhythmic patterns that are reused in multiple plays, but also because these patterns form larger standard modules (shōdan) that are found in various plays, where they serve similar structural and dramatic functions. There are about one hundred different types of shōdan that can be categorized into four types: Spoken, Chanted, Entrance and Exit music, and Dance music. For more information and video examples please refer to the Catalog of Shōdan.

The Form of Hashitomi

Zeami’s formal design should be viewed as a reference rather than an absolute, since few plays follow it thoroughly. Hashitomi and Kokaji’s form clearly underlines this point since, in their own way, they both somewhat deviate from Zeami’s model.

This figure summarizes Hashitomi's form. It differs from Zeami’s model primarily with the missing ‘Shite Performs’ part in the first act, and its Kuse appearing in the Second rather than the First Act, two differences that are not unique to Hashitomi.
