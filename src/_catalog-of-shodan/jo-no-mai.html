Catalog of Shōdan: Dance

Jo no mai

Percussion
Strict
Nohkan
Congruent

This refined and calm dance is usually presented by a shite impersonating an old man or a lady, as it is the case with Hashitomi. The complete dance includes five sections, while the abbreviated version has three. The version performed in the play Hashitomi as it appears on this website, includes all five.

The jo no mai has a three-layered structure articulated by the nohkan, percussion instruments and the choreography of the dance. The nohkan provides a cyclical melodic background, and through its recurring patterns it articulates the dance’s five parts: jo, kakari, and three sections.

The beginning of Hashitomi's jo no mai is preceded by a one-honji bridge from the preceding shōdan, the kuse. The non-congruent jiutai, accompanied by the two percussion instruments in flexible setting, introduces the first line of the poem Genji wrote in response to Lady Yūgao’s poem. The jiutai extends the poem’s last vowel over the next honji overlapping with the beginning of the jo no mai.

The name jo-no-mai stems from its introductory section called jo, which can be translated as introduction. The section stands apart from the rest of the dance as well as from other instrumental dances as the only part that includes a non-congruent nohkan, percussion in flexible rhythm. The short, only three honji-long section, is also clearly concluded by a very characteristic Left Stamp of the dancer on the seventh beat of the last honji.

Kakari is the noun derived from the verb kakaru, which means to initiate or to get into something. In the context of the jo no mai it could be understood as getting into the congruent-mode phase of the dance, thus being its second introduction after jo.

It is in the kakari that the nohkan introduces the dance’s core sequence of patterns called Ji or "ground". It is composed of four eight-beat patterns, each one beginning on a second-and-a-half beat of its corresponding honji. They are respectively labelled: Chū, Kan, Kan no Chū, and Ryo, where Kan no Chū is either a variation or repetition of Chū. The melodic shape produced by their sequencing is a four-honji long sine wave with the Chū pattern in medium range, Kan in the higher range, then back to the medium range with the Kan no Chū, before reaching the lower range with the Ryo. The Ji pattern is played four times in this kakari.

Each of the three sections that follows includes two parts. Part-I introduces a phrase composed of a short sequence of new and recurring nohkan patterns. For instance, Part-I of the First section introduces a sequence of five patterns: Dan, Kan no Chū, Oroshi, Kan no Chū, and Ryo. The structure of Part-I of the Second section is almost identical except that the Oroshi is preceded by a Ryo: Dan, Kan no Chū, Ryo, Oroshi, Kan no Chū, and Ryo. They are all eight-beat long except for the Oroshi which lasts sixteen beats. The structure of the Third section’s Part-I is slightly different, its sequence of patterns being: Dan, Chū, Ryo, where all patterns are eight-beat long except for the Dan which lasts sixteen beats.

All Part-II are compriosed of two Ji plus one or two additional patterns. The structure of the jo no mai as performed by the Kongō school can be summarized as:

First section

The instrumental version of the jo no mai included in this Catalog of shōdan starts with the Bridge from the kuse, followed by the jo's three honji, labelled Intro: 1, 2, 3, respectively, which leads to four presentations of the Ji pattern.
