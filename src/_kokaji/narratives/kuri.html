Form
First Act - Shite Performs:
Kuri → Sashi → Kuse
Synopsis
The young boy tells the story about two great Chinese Emperors and their famous swords.
Text
Arhythmic poetry.
Music
Chant of the jiutai in tsuyogin style of a text set in einori, is accompanied by percussion in strict and the nohkan in non-congruent rhythmic settings. This is the first use of tsuyogin style of singing in the play.
Visuals
The shite walks toward center stage and kneel, while the waki kneels at Square 6. For the final line of text they turn toward each other after which, the shite turns toward the audience.

This einori chant marks the beginning of a sequence of three shōdan celebrating the glorious performance of various swords from China and Japan. The decrease in the Intermedia Index is the result of the return to non-congruent singing with both actors remaining nearly motionless almost throughout the shōdan. Yet, the two characters’ close sitting proximity and their synchronized kneeling down motion could imply a building of trust. The feeling is further reinforced at the conclusion of the shōdan when they turn to face each other.

The fast and upbeat music featuring the percussion instruments in strict rhythmic setting and a two-pattern non-congruent nohkan part, is well suited for a text that recounts the exploits of two swords: the Chinese Three Foot and Kei swords. The story of the former loosely coinciding the nohkan’s first pattern, the story of the latter with its second one. From this point on in the play, the light singing mode yowagin is replaced with the heavier tsuyogin, which is commonly used to express bravery and excitement.
