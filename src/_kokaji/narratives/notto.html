Form
Second Act - Waki Waits:
Notto → Noriji-1
Synopsis
Munechika expresses reverence for the masters that gave him his craft.
Text
Arhythmic poetry.
Music
The shōdan is divided into two parts: notto-music, followed by the notto-chant. The notto-music for percussion in strict and nohkan in non-congruent rhythmic setting. It is followed by the notto-chant where the nohkan is replaced with a sashinori chant sung in tsuyogin style by the waki.
Visuals
The waki walks back on stage and sits on Square 8, that is in front of the hayashi as it performs the notto-music. As it is about to start the notto-chant, he walks to front-stage where a tsukurimono representing his forge is set. After mounting it, he kneels and picks up a sacred wand.

The first act opens right away in the medium range of the Intermedia Index. It feels as if the drama picks up where it left off at the end of the first act, and continues on a steady course of increasing activity even within the shōdan itself. All of the instruments are playing from the beginning, some with a regularity of pulse. The waki first joins by performing mimetic movements, before starting to sing.

The notto begins with a lively musical introduction. The percussion parts are in strict rhythmic setting. The kotsuzumi player sets the tone by playing the steady notto uchitsume pattern characterized by a stroke on each of the honji’s eight beats. Both the kotsuzumi pattern and the non-congruent nohkan’s part are exclusively used in the notto-music.

Munechika has been waiting in a kneeling position at Square 8, facing diagonally away from the audience, as if absent. About half-way into the introduction music, he stands up and walks towards the dais representing his forge, steps on it and kneels. Picking up a sacred wand with the right hand, he performs a purifying ritual. The text of the notto-chant, set insashinori is loosely synchornized with the accompaniment by fitting its lines between the chon strokes of the ōtsuzumi. These fall on honji’s third beat and are preceded by a slight slowdown and an extended kakegoe. Longer lines that do not fit within the eight-beat span of the successive chon strokes, are spread over three or four honji. In this case, there is no rallentando leading to the intermediate chon stroke(s), and during the intermediate honji the ōtsuzumi performs a sequence of four, five, or six chon strokes that maintain the momentum.

From time to time the chant slows down to emphasize selected words. This happens for instance, when Munechika acknowledges that his craft came to him through no effort of his own with the line ‘chikara ni arazu. The emphasis can also be done by a combination of rhythm and pitch. When Munechika raises the wand and prays for divine assistance in the completion his task, the line lasts three honji and its syllables are spread to create a rallentando on the words: “...I make my petition”. The stress on the keyword ‘sasage’ is emphasized when Munechika interrupts his steady tone with a raising of pitch followed by a descending glissando. In this case, the effect could be perceived as an expression of commitment toward the fulfillment of the imperial order.
