Form
First Act - Dialogue:
Mondō-2 → Ageuta-2
Synopsis
Munechika is told to have faith and forge the requested sword.
Text
Poetry in regular rhythm.
Music
Chant of the jiutai in yowagin style, set in hiranori with accompaniment of hayashi. The percussion is in strict and the nohkan in non-congruent rhythmic setting.
Visuals
This shite performs some basic kata and a modest opening of the stage space around Square 1.

The Dialogue dan is concluded with a congruent chant that substantially increases the Intermedia Index. It is delivered by the jiutai, who speaks for the young boy. The poetic text set in hiranori accompanied by percussion set in strict rhythm and a non-congruent nohkan's part, carries the boy’s words of encouragements for Munechika. The dramatic expression is very much heightened by the use of formal kata that align well with the musical changes but not at all with the boy’s words. Against his encouraging words sung by the jiutai, the movements performed by the two characters appear to display mistrust and psychological tension between them.

Munechika is clearly uneasy about meeting an unknown boy who knows much about him. He is not reassured when told that ”Walls have ears: In a world where rocks may speak…”. The distrust between the two characters is made clear when they break eye contact and turn away from each other. This happens during the uchikiri which separate the ageuta’s initial line from its repetition. The powerful kakegoe ‘iya’ and the nohkan’s hishigi takane mi kusari pattern, energize the music and carry the melodic line that follows the repetition into the higher range, where the corresponding words end up emphasized. In this case, the emphasized text kakure which means ‘hidden’, maintains the feeling of distrust as shown by the fact that the two protagonists continue to keep their distance from one another.

It is upon hearing a reference to the "radiance of the Emperor’s sword" that the two characters turn towards each another, attempting one more time to establish trust. The shite even goes as far as to take three steps towards the waki, but the effort is in vain, and understood as failed when the shite turns away from the waki and circles to Square 1. The nohkan underlines the tense situation with the entrance of the naka no takane pattern.

Finally, after rebuilding momentum with a Forward Pointing and an Open-retreat, it is over the words “It (the sword) must be realized” that the two protagonists once again turn towards each other, and that the shite takes One Step towards the waki, suggesting that the communication line between the two is now open.

Although done in a much subtler fashion, the changes in the music structure follow the unfolding narrative. The three attempts at setting up trust between the boy and the swordsmith are matched with three changes in the delivery mode from the declamatory mitsuji-utai used for setting up the attempt, to the rhythmic tsuzuke-utai used for the first two unsuccessful consequences and the last propitious one:

#1a – Mitsuji-utai
In a world where rocks may speak,
#1b – Tsuzuke-utai
Nothing is hidden.
#2a – Mitsuji-utai
…Nothing shall dim the radiance…
#2b – Tsuzuke-utai
…of the Emperor’s sword.
#3a – Mitsuji-utai
Only have faith…
#3b – Tsuzuke-utai
through his Majesty’s grace and sword…
