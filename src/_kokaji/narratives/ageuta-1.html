Form
First Act - Waki Enters:
Nanori → Mondō-1 → Ageuta-1 → Dialogue
Synopsis
Inner monologue of Munechika. With hesitation about his ability to forge the blade but in deference to emperor’s envoy he resolves to trust that a miracle will happen.
Text
Poetry in regular rhythm.
Music
After an opening line by the waki, the jiutai takes over the chanting in yowagin style of a text set in hiranori. They are accompanied by percussion in strict and the nohkan in non-congruent rhythmic settings. It is the first entrance in the play of the jiutai and of the full hayashi.
Visuals
The action begins with the wakizure standing at the First Pine and the waki kneeling and bowing at Third Pine. It is followed by the first expansion onto the stage-space proper with the two performers walking to Squares 5 and 9, respectively.

The third shōdan of the Waki Enters dan is its high-point. It corresponds to a sudden and substantial expansion of the musical texture, with the first entrance of the jiutai, the two hand-percussion instruments and the nohkan. It is also characterized by an increase in the use of stage-space, with both actors moving from the bridge to the main stage. The shōdan also differs from the preceding two with its text setting. Up to this point, the text delivered in a spoken fashion, introduced factual information, but for the span of this ageuta the focus switches to the Munechika’s inner feelings expressed in sung poetry set in hiranori.

Most of Kokaji’s chants are sung in the strong tsuyogin style, except for its two ageuta. They both carry Munechika’s expression of hesitation and self-doubt, and are sung in the more lyrical yowagin style.

Typically, the first line of text of an ageuta is repeated. Here, the line is first delivered by Munechika and repeated by the jiutai, who thus becomes Munechika's voice, sharing his inner feelings with the audience. As it is standard, the initial line and its repetition are separated by an uchikiri, joined in this case by the nohkan takane pattern. It energizes the music and carries the melodic line that follows the repetition into the higher range, where the corresponding words end up emphasized. In the case of this ageuta, the high range is reached over the words shintai, which could be translated as “must decide”, showing Munechika to be under tremendous pressure.

The entrance of the next nohkan pattern, the naka no takane anticipates a shift in text setting that reflects Munechika's changing mindset. The declamatory mitsuji-utai used in the first part to express Munechika's self-doubting feeling, is replaced with the rhytmical tsuzuke-utai, a change that could suggest that he has faith in divine help.

The ageuta corresponds also with the first expansion of the use of stage space. Up to this point, it had been limited to the Bridge and Square 1, but as the jiutai carries the chant, both characters walk from the bridge to the stage. Closer to the beginning of the shōdan’s second part, the envoy kneels at Square 5, the traditional position for a secondary actor, while Munechika stops center-stage at Square 9, turns and kneels facing the audience. By occupying the strong central position, which is usually reserved to the shite, we can surmise that Munechika’s role is of greater importance than most waki's.
