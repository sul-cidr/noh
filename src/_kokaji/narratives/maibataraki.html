Form
Second Act - Shite Performs:
Noriji-2 → Maibataraki → Noriji-3
Synopsis
The deity shows off its power through dance.
Text
None.
Music
All three percussion instruments in strict and the nohkan in congruent rhythmic settings.
Visuals
Dynamic short instrumental dance using most of the stage space. Waki facing to the back of the stage prepares for the following scene of blade forging by lifting the cloak from his right arm.

The maibataraki has the highest Intermedia Index in the play. It is a very fast and energetic dance accompanied by congruent nohkan with all percussion instruments in strict rhythm. Whereas the kuse and kiri are dance-to-text, the maibataraki is an instrumental dance based primarily on abstract kata that are nonetheless quite expressive. The word maibataraki is a combination of mai, which signifies pure dance and hataraki, which refers to mimetic dances from the early days of Noh. In Kokaji’s case, it may be understood as a scene in which the deity descends from heavens and shows off his power before taking part in the forging of the sword.

Musically, the dance has a three-section structure: kakari, ji, and dan. It is articulated by the nohkan's recurring patterns synchronized to the pulsated background of three percussion instruments. The taiko, uses its distinguishable head pattern kashira for the beginning and closing of cycles. The dance structure loosely matches the musical one with the kakari serving as a stamping introduction, ji for opening the stage-space leading to the overtaking of its powerful center and dan serving to offer a formal closure and return toward the back.

Please note that because of great complexity but limited space on the page the percussion score shows only the taiko part.

Kakari

The kakari, identified in the score with the abbreviation 'Ka', is two- honji long and matches the introductory stamping of the shite. The word kakari can be translated as ‘taking the time to perform something’ or 'initiating something'. In the case of the maibataraki, it is used to set the dance’s dynamic and upbeat mood. Stamps are customarily performed by the shite at the beginning of a dance, as if calling the listener to attention. The dance opens with a sequence of Six Stamps, each one in phase with one of the percussionists’ strokes. The first one is also in phase with the very first note of the nohkan, which plays in a congruent setting for the second and last time in the play.

Ji

The form of this section, identified in the score with the abbreviation 'Ji', is musically lead by the nohkan’s patterns, while spatially, it corresponds to an opening of the stage-space and imposing control over the center. The nohkan introduces its core sequence of three patterns that we labelled in the score: a, b, and c, each one lasting one honji. The sequence is played twice. It opens and closes with distinguishable kashira patterns of the taiko.

Against this temporal framework the shite traverses first the right lateral (Squares 1 to 3), then the front stage (Square 3 to 5), and finally the left-right transversal (Square 5 to 8). This trajectory does not only open-up the stage-space, it also creates a spatial wave of tension-release. The backstage positions of Squares 1 and 8, being positions of rest, they are often used as starting and closing positions for a dance and its various sections. On the other hand, front-stage positions of Squares 3,4,5 and the center-stage Square 9, offer strength and energy. Therefore, in the sequence the motion Square 1 to 3 builds momentum, Square 3 to 5 maintains it, whereas Square 5 to 8 releases tension. It is interesting to note that the above sequence of expansion of the stage-space in the maibataraki is identical to Hashitomi’s jonomai’s second section.

Dan

The temporal boundaries of this four-honji closing section, identified in the score with the abbreviation 'Cl', are once again clearly articulated by the hayashi. The nohkan initiates it with two new one-honji patterns (that we labelled 'd', and 'e' in the score) followed by a return of the pattern ‘a’ and the cadential pattern tome. The dan’s four honji also coincide with the taiko’s second cycle, with its duration once again deliniated by the kashira patterns. Finally, the taiko’s dropping out in the dan’s first honji creates a sudden change of density that helps distinguish its beginning.

During the dan, the shite occupies center-stage where he performs a series of impressive kata that reinforce his image of a powerful being: The sequence starts with Two Large Steps and a Jump Kneel, followed by a Double-Kneel, and a Flip Left Sleeve. This is followed with a Right Circlet and a Circle back to Square 1, where he performs an Open-retreat, a kata often used as a closing gesture.
