Form
First Act - Shite Performs:
Kuri → Sashi → Kuse
Synopsis
An account given by the young boy of victorious battles of prince Yamato-dake and the power of his sword. Munechika is told to be confident about accomplishing his task.
Text
Poetry in irregular rhythm.
Music
The text set in hiranori is sung in tsuyogin style by the jiutai with three interventions by the shite. The percussion is in strict and the nohkan in non-congruent rhythmic settings.
Visuals
In the first of three parts, the shite continues to kneel on Square 9 turning toward the waki only for the closing line. He then turns back and begins the second part ornamented by a sequence of mimetic movements while remaining in a kneeling position. The third part consists of an active dance involving the full stage. This is the first dance-to-text of the play.

This shōdan represents the dramatic highlight of the first act, as it recounts heroic battles and introduces the play’s first substantial dance-to-text section. The poetic text of the hiranori chant is sung by the jiutai who speaks for the boy, and the shite whose three ageha lines are interspersed within. It is accompanied by the percussion instruments in strict rhythmic setting and three entrances of a non-congruent nohkan part. These bring the Intermedia Index almost to the highest possible value. The points of intermedia interest are in the use of music that helps articulate the narrative’s meaning and structure, and the use of mimetic movement that illustrates the narrative while providing a gradual formal build-up.

The text tells the story of first century Yamato-dake, son of legendary Emperor Keikō, and the exploits of his sword Kusanagi no tsurugi. Based on the narrative and use of the dance space, the kuse can be divided into two parts. An introductory first part during which the shite remains kneeling in center stage, and a danced second part that recounts the exploits of the Kusanagi no tsurugi illustrated by various mimetic kata.

The first part develops its narrative through stages, which are deliniated by the salient three agehas of the shite and two entrances of the nohkan. In the context of the rather static first part small and rare movements take particular significance.

The first lines of text introduce Yamato-dake. Once the action starts and we hear about him going East to subdue the barbarians, the perilous journey is highlighted by the nohkan’s first entrance with the naka no takane pattern. After we learn that Yamato-dake yearned to come home the music slows down and in the first ageha he stresses being constantly kept away by battles.

The tempo picks up as images of the battlefield with gore details are referenced, hightenned by the nohkan’s second entrance and the takane mi kusari pattern. As the description is concluding the boy makes his very first move as he slides left to face Munechika, thus reminding all that it is for his sake that this story is being told. A slow-down and transitional uchikiri prepare for an introduction to the next battle.

We are told that at the time the autumnal colors were fading away. The shite who now stands for Yamato-dake slides to the right, grabs his forelock, raises on his knee, and stares in the distance at the fading fall colors. But the circumstances will soon become dire. Right after the second ageha in which Yamato-dake recounts being surrounded by barbarians we learn that the enemies set the grass around him on fire. His Right-Left-Right Head motion shows him assessing the danger. The intensity of the moment is underscored by the nohkan’s hishigi takane mi kusari pattern, its last entrance. A moment later, his Downward Head motion, as the jiutai refers to the “enemies’ beating war drums” suggests that the warrior is considering his options. Finally, as the jiutai refers to “…the towering flames as the attack blazed forward." Yamato-dake who is about to drew his sword, is assessing the situation for the last time as he half rises on his knee and faces front, then right.

The kuse’s second part showcases Yamato-dake’s sword prowess. Helped by the kotsuzumi's patterns which incorporate up-beat strokes, the overall tempo of this part is faster than the preceding one and the mimetic dance is activated to its fullest.

As the shite sings that “the Prince drew his sword ”, he uses his fan as a sword that he points straight in front of him. He then stands up and takes four steps towards the strong Square 4, and performs two standard kata often used at the beginning of a dance: Forward Pointing and Open-retreat. He continues to use his fan as a sword lashing it left and right over the words “As it mowed down the grasses in all directions”.

As he performs a sequence of Four Stamps, he Opens the Fan, thus marking the start of the dance’s high point. He takes a series of steps that bring him from front to back-stage (Square 8), temporarily relaxing the tension. From this point on, the shite starts rebuilding momentum by performing a Scooping Point that carries him back to the strong Square 4 where he performs a Right Circlet to mimic the words “The blaze lit up the heavens, covered the earth! ”.

Upon hearing the words “A furnace of fierce flames, turned back to devour the enemy forces”, he moves to the very strong Square 3, glances Left-Right-Left-Right-Down at the enemy and uses his fan as if aiming the flames towards the barbarians, with a Pointing to the Right as he is Taking the Corner. Then, he starts a Large Circle to the Left, but stops on Square 5 to perform a Scooping point followed by a Single Right Stamp that marks a sudden slowdown in the tempo, that announces the dance’s imminent conclusion. It is over the words “So in later years”, that the speed drops, suggesting that the narration of Yamato-dake’s exploits is over. On his way back to Square 8, the shite Closes his Fan confirming that the dance is coming to an end, and upon reaching his destination, he concludes the dance commemorating Yamato-dake’s exploits with the standard sequence of Scooping Point, and an Open-retreat.

The words “And now…” confirms that the narration of Yamato-dake’s story is indeed over and that we are returning to the present, hence it is the words of the young boy that the jiutai directs at Munechika. As the jiutai intones “And now, this auspiciously omened blade that you must forge! ”, the tempo gradually increases to underline the urgency of the task ahead.

Finally, the boy turns towards Munechika and takes one step towards him but as if feeling that he must stress his point again and more strongly, he turns back and goes to Square 1. After pivoting to face Munechika, he performs a Scooping Point and while pointing with the fan in an insisting fashion he Takes Six Steps towards the swordsmith just as the jiutai sings “Munechika, heir to a house of proud tradition, set your heart at rest and go home now! ”. The tempo having gradually slowed down to its lowest, the boy standing center-stage, concludes the kuse with an Open-retreat and Kneels. He finishes the shōdan at the same place and same position as he started.
