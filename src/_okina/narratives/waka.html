Form
Second Act - Shite Exits:
Waka → Kiri
Synopsis
The spirit of Lady Yūgao re-starts reciting the poem with which Genji replied to Lady Yūgao.
Text
Poetry in regular rhythm.
Music
The einori chant performed by the shite is accompanied by the percussion instruments in strict rhythmic setting. The nohkan concludes the jonomai by performing its non-congruent cadential tome pattern over the waka's first honji.
Visuals
The shite remains almost motionless on Square 9.

TThe very short Waka is both a contrast and a bridge between the two major shōdan: the Jonomai and the Kiri. It releases tension built by the intense aestheticism of the instrumental dance and prepares for a narrative ending.

After the instrumental dance Jonomai, rich in use of various movements and extensive stage-space, the intermedia richness temporarily drops with the Waka, as the shite performs a single kata, a held Raised Fan. He does so standing in center stage, which allows him to retain energy and exert command of the stage. The music, which maintains the dynamic percussion strict rhythmic setting, brings back chanting. The melisma of shite's non-congruent einori chant extends the dance's aesthetic focus and recalls the highly ornamented slow nohkan melody.

The percussion part forms another bridge between two rhythmically congruent but divergent shōdan. The slow speed of the preceding Jonomai gave it a free-flowing temporal feeling that was well-suited for the dance of an elegant young lady. It is in clear contrast with the steady-beat of the ōnori chant of the coming Kiri. In the Waka, the drums start with two honji of broad and sparse mitsuji patterns then, accelerate toward the last two honji of tsuzuke patterns. At the same time the initial extended kakegoe of the ōtsuzumi player that seemed to imply a flexible setting are answered by short kakegoe of the kotsuzumi player. By the time they play tsuzuke patterns they are both in strict rhythm, setting up the beat for the Kiri's ōnori chant.
