Form
First Act - Shite Exits:
Kakaru-2 → Ageuta-1
Synopsis
The monk continues to ask the apparition about her identity. She reveals she is no longer alive but that she could always be found at a certain mansion.
Text
Prose and arhythmic poetry.
Music
An exchange between the waki and shite alternates between kotoba and sashinori chant, accompanied by the kotsuzumi and ōtsuzumi’s parts in a flexible rhythmic setting.
Visuals
The use of stage-space remains static with the shite standing at Square 1, and the waki kneeling at Square 5. The shite alternates between facing the waki and the audience.

Since the exchange between the actors is now accompanied, there is a slight increase in the Intermedia Index. The percussion instruments play in flexible setting, offering a slow, sparse and free-flowing accompaniment that complements the unmetered vocal parts. The monk, singing a chant set in sashinori, , insists that the shite reveals her name. The shite resists and, as though to stress her position, changes mode of delivery and replies in kotoba. However, while keeping her name secret, she confirms that she is no longer alive . The Kakaru concludes with the following two lines:

“At a certain mansion,
I could always be found, indeed it is so.”

In the Genji monogatari, Lady Yūgao’s dwelling was surrounded by a fence filled with ‘evening face’ flowers. Although at this point in the play the actual identity of the shite’s character is still unknown, the monk speculates about it. The first line of the above sentence, which should be attributed to the apparition, is actually sung by the priest, who may have a hunch about her identity. When she sings the remaining line, she turns towards the monk, as if acknowledging his intuition. This confirmation coincides with a change in the character of the percussion material, which moves from sparse mitsuji to the regular tsuzuke pattern. The switch subtly increases the density of the shōdan and raises expectations for the one to come.
