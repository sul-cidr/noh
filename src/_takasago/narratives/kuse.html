Form
First Act - Shite Performs:
Kuri → Sashi 2 → Kuse
Synopsis
The jiutai and the old man discuss the relationship between nature and poetry. They begin with the words of Chōnō, who remarked that both sentient and insentient things in nature conceal feelings and embody poetry.
Text
Poetry in irregular rhythm.
Music
The chant set in hiranori is sung in tsuyogin style by the jiutai with one line (ageha) by the shite. It is accompanied by the percussion in strict and nohkan in non-congruent rhythmic settings.
Visuals
The shite, kneeling at Square 9, initially faces front but slides to the left to face the waki when the text references various sentient and insentient things, asking, 'Do they not embody poetry?' He slides a second time to face the waki as the text praises the virtues of Emperor Shikō. After delivering the ageha, the shite stands up and performs various mimetic kata related to the sung text.

Intermedia analysis

The dance divides the shōdan into two parts: the kneeling part and the standing part, which begins at the ageha. The text focuses on Takasago and celebrates poetry, one of the play’s central themes.

Sitting part

An uchikiri divides this section into two sub-parts. The first consists of three phrase-waves: tori – mitsu-ji – tsuzuke, which lead up to the uchikiri. This structure creates waves of increasing momentum. It begins with a tori (a four-beat measure), followed by a mitsu-ji (an eight-beat measure with only a few stroked beats), and then a tsuzuke (another eight-beat measure, this time with most beats stroked). The gradually increasing number of pulsated beats, along with the accelerated tempo, gives the music an energetic feel suited to the text’s celebratory tone.

The text centers on the theme of poetry, enumerating various insentient entities such as plants, trees, and the elemental sounds of wind and waves—several of which bear a strong relationship to poetic expression, as exemplified by terms like So-o-mo-ku-do-sha (grass, wood, soil, sand) and fu-u-sei-su-i-on (voice of wind and sound of water). The text also employs a distinctive technique to foreground particular words: the four-beat measure known as tori. For instance, the phrase ha-ru-no ha-ya-shi-no, consisting of seven syllables, is distributed across two tori. Given that a single one can accommodate up to eight syllables, the sixteen available positions in two tori allow for intentional pauses between words, thereby enhancing their emphasis. In this context, the respective words for “spring” and “forest” are further accentuated through the application of the nohkan’s naka no takane pattern. This process of enumeration and emphasis culminates in the central question: “Do they not embody poetry?” The play’s thematic focus on poetry is further underscored when the shite turns to the left to face the waki while posing this question.

The nohkan’s naka no takane pattern marks the beginning of the second half, which introduces a similar sequence of phrase-waves. This time, the text focuses on the Takasago pine tree. Following an enumeration of its most remarkable attributes, the shite once again slides left to face the waki as the jiutai, speaking for him, states, “All praise its virtues.” This statement is further underlined by the nohkan’s mi roku no ge pattern.

Standing part

This part begins with the shite’s ageha. It is characterized by a sudden change in vocal density—from eight voices to one—a sharp decrease in tempo, and a shift in the text, which now describes the landscape surrounding the magnificent Takasago tree. The return of the jiutai as lead singer is emphasized by the nohkan’s hishigi takane mi kusari pattern.

As the text alludes to the pine’s fallen leaves, the shite performs the ‘raking the leaves’ kata. According to the philologist Dr. Setauko Otani, this gesture also symbolizes the prosperity of the reign but also the prosperity of poetry that should be regarded as parallel with the eternity of the reign. In other words, at this point, text and kata bring in synchronicity two of the play’s three celebrated themes.

In the third phrase-wave, the deep green color of the leaves is said to symbolize a long-lasting reign, referencing one of the play’s main topics. Approaching its conclusion, the text introduces praise for Takasago and reverence for the ‘Twin Paired’, one of the play’s three honored themes. In the fourth phrase-wave, the shite moves away from center stage for the first time in the shōdan, going to Square 1. Having captured our attention, he performs a Right circlet, a Forward point, and an Open-retreat as the jiutai sings: “Among ever-green trees, the name of Takasago stands out…”, returning the shōdan’s focus to its main subject, Takasago.

The shite then returns to Square 9, kneeling and facing the waki as the jiutai sings, “…through generations its paired pines are the image of this blessing,” a praise of the ‘Twin Pines’ emphasized by the shite’s stage motion, the nohkan’s mi roku no ge pattern, and two percussionists, who bring the shōdan to a close with a two-measure uchikiri.
