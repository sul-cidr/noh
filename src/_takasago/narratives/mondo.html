Synopsis
In this dialogue, the priest asks why the Takasago and Suminoe pines are considered a "pair," despite being geographically distant from one another. In response, the old man refers to them as the "Paired Pines," a term inspired by the preface of the Kokinshu, contemporary to the action, early Heian period anthology of waka poetry.

Prose.
Music
Unaccompanied exchange between the shite and waki delivered in kotoba.
Visuals
Facing front, the shite and shitezure stand on Squares 8 and 2, respectively, while the waki stands at Square 5, facing the shite. The dialogue occurs exclusively between the shite and the waki, with the shite turning toward the waki when responding to his questions. While singing about the Takasago pine, he slides slightly in front-left direction.

This very simple scene features a dialogue between two characters with minimal motion and no music. However, it conveys a meaningful contrast in age and implied status: the god character sings in a lower pitch and slower tempo, while the waki speaks in a faster, more natural style.
