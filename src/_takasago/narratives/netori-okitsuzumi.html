Visuals
The waki dressed in humble priest clothes CORRECT LINK enters the stage, stops at Pine 3 and bows to the audience before moving to Square 8. He is followed by two wakizure who walk to Square 1.

On very special occasions—such as in our recording—and particularly when Takasago follows Okina, the traditional Shidai music is replaced by an entrance piece called Netori Okitsuzumi. Although the term netori traditionally denotes tuning music, here it becomes an elaborate display of melodic patterns that accompanies the actors’ formal entrance and greeting.

Netori Okitsuzumiis distinctive in using only two instruments—the kotsuzumi and the nohkan. At first, they alternate entrances: as the nohkan plays shifting melodic patterns, the kotsuzumi responds with a recurring figure spread over two honji, which we label A-A’. After two such exchanges, with the nohkan’s phrases growing progressively shorter, the third entrance has the kotsuzumi join the nohkan. During the fourth solo entrance of the nohkan, the curtain opens; as the kotsuzumi joins, the waki and two wakizure make a slow, dignified entrance.

The celebratory quality of the waki’s entrance and that of his cohort is underlined by a sequence of three formal gestures, of which normally only the last would suffice. Immediately upon entering, the waki performs the Catch the Entrance kata, a formal appearance appropriate in a First Category play. He then offers the patrons a deeply respectful bow. Finally, he performs the tappai kata, gathering his sleeves and drawing his arms together in prayer.

The shōdan divides into three short, purely musical sections, followed by a longer fourth section that accompanies stage action:

I The nohkan opens the shōdan with a Ryo focusing on its lower register, followed by a Kan pattern that moves to the higher register. The kotsuzumi then introduces its pattern: A-A’.
II The nohkan performs the Naka no takane pattern, followed by the kotsuzumi’s A-A’ pattern.
III The nohkan begins with the Takane hane pattern, this time soon joined by the kotsuzumi’s A-A’ pattern.
IV The final section starts again with the nohkan alone. Soon after, the curtain is raised as the nohkan continues a series of patterns. As the waki and his attendants enter, the kotsuzumi performs a sequence of repetitive patterns: A-A’, A-A-A-A’, A-A”-A’, B-A’, where "B" is performed faster and functions as a cadential figure, preparing the final pattern and a slowdown.
