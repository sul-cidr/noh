When Takasago follows Okina and during the special occasion of this recording, the traditional Shidai music is replaced by an entrance piece known as Netori Okitsuzumi. This composition is distinctive in that it features only two instruments: the kotsuzumi and the nohkan. The interaction between these two instruments is one of equals. At first, they alternate entrances; the kotsuzumi maintains a recognizable four-measure recurring pattern, while the nohkan weaves in constantly shifting melodies. After three pairs of entrances, each featuring a progressively shorter phrase from the nohkan, the two instruments merge, signaling the opening of the curtain. The kotsuzumi then pauses, allowing the nohkan to take the lead, which introduces the entrance of the Waki. The kotsuzumi re-enters as the Waki and his two attendants continue their ceremonial entrance.

At measure 23, immediately following his entrance, the Waki performs the Catch the Entrance kata, an act of bending and straightening appropriate for the First Category play. By measure 26, he greets the audience with a deeply respectful bow. At measure 31, he executes the tappai kata, a formal gesture of collecting his sleeves, further enhancing the elegance of his greeting.

Interestingly, while the name netori traditionally refers to tuning music, in this context, it transforms into an intricate showcase of multiple melodic patterns.
