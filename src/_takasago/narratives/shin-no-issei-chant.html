Form
First Act - Shite Enters:
Shin no Issei Music → Shin no Issei Chant → Sashi 1 → Sageuta → Ageuta 1
Synopsis
The old man and old woman comment on the spring wind blowing through the pines, the evening sounds of bells from the peak of Onoe, and the ocean waves hidden in a haze.
Text
Poetic text in regular rhythm.
Music
The music is structured into four sections. First three are a non-congruent chant performed in einori, utilizing the tsuyogin style. Following these, there is a purely instrumental segment known as ashirai. The nohkan is featured in both the initial section of the chant and the instrumental ashirai, maintaining a non-congruent setting throughout. In contrast, the percussion plays in a strict rhythmic setting during the chant but adopts the flexible rhythmic setting during the ashirai segment. The first and third sections are chanted slowly by the shite and shitezure, while the middle section is performed more rapidly by the shitezure alone, on a higher pitch.
Visuals
The two actors first perform while facing each other, positioned at the 3rd and 1st Pines Pine. They subsequently turn to the audience for the ninoku, during which only the shitezure sings, before returning to face one another again. The shōdan concludes with an ashirai, as both actors enter the stage; the wakizure moves first and slightly faster, walking to Square 9, while the shite goes to Square 1. Initially, both actors carry their rake and broom on their shoulders, but during the ashirai section, the shite lowers his rake.
CAN WE SAY SOMETHING ABOUT THEIR COSTUME?

The shin no issei chant demonstrates a rich interplay of vocal styles, movement, and staging conventions. The sequence begins as the shite (old man) and shitezure (old woman) sing together about the wind blowing through the pines as day draws to a close. Their singing is a slow, low-pitched einori chant, characterized by great expressive intensity and extended melismas. Facing each other, the actors’ bodies display deep attention and connection, and their performance is enhanced by the soulful sound of the nohkan flute.

As their duet concludes, the singing slows dramatically on extended melismatic notes. Just before the lyrics refer to the resonance of bells, the nohkan plays the hibiki no te ("resonance") pattern. The actors then turn to the front for the ninoku ("the second phrase"), a classical segment typical of two-actor issei chants in First Category plays. It provides a contrast that enriches the performance and enhances its celebratory character. The shitezure sings this part alone at a faster pace and higher pitch, exposed by the absence of the nohkan accompaniment. A two-measure percussion solo precedes her entrance, beginning slowly to create space before accelerating to set the new tempo. This contrast enhances the principal actor’s presence and emotional depth and is perceived as a celebration of Yin and Yang. This duality is further marked by their costumes, contrasting inflections of movement, and the way they carry their broom and rake—one directed up, the other down.

As the chant concludes, the nohkan re-enters to lead the instrumental ashirai section, during which both actors move to the main stage. The shitezure moves first and more quickly to Square 9, thereby preparing or "opening" the space and time for the shite's prominence. The shite follows at a slower pace to Square 1. Notably, the shite does not take the stronger position of Square 9 because it is customary for the principal actor to deliver his first song of the play from Square 1. He will occupy the whole stage, including Square 9, later in the performance.
