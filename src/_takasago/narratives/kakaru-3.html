Form
Second Act - Waki Waits:
Kakaru-3
Synopsis
The monk arrives at Gojō to visit the one-time home of Lady Yūgao. He observes that the dwelling is still covered by the same yūgao flowers but overgrown with weeds. He begins to recite a poem that references spiritual learning and poverty.
Text
Arhythmic poetry.
Music
Sashinori chant delivered by the waki in yowagin style, accompanied by the kotsuzumi and ōtsuzumi’s parts in a flexible rhythmic setting.
Visuals
After kneeling at Square 5, the waki stands up, and turns towards the tsukurimono, which represents Lady Yūgao's dwelling. He takes two steps in its direction, indicating that he has made the trip to Gojō, where Lady Yūgao lived.

A tsukurimono stands on Square 8, representing what used to be Lady Yūgao’s dwelling. The waki sings, standing on Square 5, facing it. This Kakaru is spread over five eight-beat measures (honji). It starts simply with the waki singing a non-congruent sashinori chant accompanied by the drums in flexible rhythmic setting. Half-way in the third honji, the kotsuzumi player initiates a switch from flexible to strict setting, a motion recognizable by his much shorter kakegoe. At this point, the percussionists also switch from the sparse mitsuji to the steadier tsuzukepatterns. This change coincides with a switch in the text from prose to verse , as the waki starts reciting a poem. It refers to a Confucian disciple and to ‘empty gourds,’ a metaphor for poverty. This foreshadows the theme of sadness that will weave through the next three shōdan.
