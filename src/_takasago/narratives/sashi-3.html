Form
Second Act - Dialogue:
Sashi 3 → Jō no ei → Issei Chant
Synopsis
Sumiyoshi's god reflects on his long devotion to the soevereign and the sacred pine of Sumiyoshi. He asks the musicans for nighttime dances accompanied by drums.
Text
Arhythmic poetry.
Music
The shite sings this sashinori chant, in tsuyogin style accompanied by a non-congruent nohkan part and three percussion instruments in strict rhythmic settings.
Visuals
The shite stands by Pine 1 to deliver his chant. At the conclusion, he performs a series of kata that signifies an increase in motion intensity, beginning with the opening of his fan and culminating in a Forward Point, which is finished with a swirling of his left sleeves.

This Sashi section is distinguished by the participation of the taiko, suggesting the presence of a supernatural being on stage, as its sound is well-suited for that role. This presence changes the typical rhythmic setting of Sashi from flexible to strict. The shōdan is highly energetic, with the tempo increasing in the second half, accompanied by the nohkan.

A unique scene unfolds in the final part of the chant, where the shite, embodying the god of Sumiyoshi, integrates the stage musicians into the dramatic world. In this fictional setting, the god commands the dancers at the Sumiyoshi shrine to perform a kagura dance, accompanied by drum beats from the musicians (measures 8-13). The shite strikes his hands twice while performing the Flap Fan kata—an action that opens and closes the arms as if encouraging the drumming—before executing a Twirl Sleeve and directly pointing to the musicians, using them to represent the fictional musicians in his narrative. Throughout this section, intense, strict rhythmic patterns led by the taiko illustrate also his final words: "(...) the drums in the night, with measured rhythm, soothe the god, celebrants of the shrine." This is further emphasized by a distinctive nohkan pattern called notarnoryo, which features a very low note.
