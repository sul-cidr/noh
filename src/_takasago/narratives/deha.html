Second Act - Shite Re-enters:
Deha
Synopsis
Introductory music and the entrance of the Sumiyoshi god.
Text
n/a
Music
Dynamic entrance music played by a nohkan in non-congruent and three percussion instruments in strict rhythmic settings.
Visuals
Close to the end of the entrance music, the nochi-shite enters. NOTES ABOUT THE COSTUME. He stops at Pine 3 and faces front to perform a Figre Eight Fan kata. Then, he moves to Pine 1, where he once again faces front and performs a Forward Point followed with an Open-retreat.

This dynamic entrance music announces the arrival of a powerful character. In the case of Takasago, it heralds the God of Sumiyoshi. The deha features a non-congruent nohkan part accompanied by the three percussion instruments in a strict rhythmic setting. This is the first shōdan of the second act to include the full hayashi. The Intermedia Index rises as the shōdan continues to elevate the play’s energy level.

The taiko part introduces five cycles of patterns that follow the standard sequence: Head, First Transition, Ground, Second Transition, and Closing. In the first four cycles, the number of measures allocated to the Head pattern decreases while the number of measures assigned to the other four segments remains unchanged: one for First Transition, three for Ground, one for Second Transition, and one for Closing. The distribution of measures changes for the fifth cycle, which essentially serves as a transitional phrase leading to the next shōdan. Twice the nohkan joins the percussion trio, each time entering during the first measure of a group of Ground patterns: the first time at measure 9 until 13, the second time at measure 34 until the end coinciding with the opening of the curtain.

The pattern commencing at measure 9 can be divided into two parts: the first part consists of the same opening pattern used in the issei music (beginning at measure 24), which concludes with a variant of the takane hane pattern. The pattern beginning at measure 34 is a variation of the second pattern also found in the issei music (also beginning at measure 24), followed by a cadential pattern played in the instrument’s lower register.

At the beginning of the nohkan's second entry, the curtain rises, and the shite makes his entrance onto the bridge. He immediately turns and faces front, performing the dynamic Figure Eight kata that showcases his divine stature. He then proceeds to the First Pine, delaying his entrance onto the main stage as he first turns to face front and then executes the standard sequence of kata: Forward Point and Open-retreat.

It should be noted that the information provided above pertains specifically to the performance of this deha, which has been adapted for this special occasion. In Japanese culture, odd numbers are generally considered auspicious, symbolizing harmony and completeness, a concept reflected in the number of kashira patterns performed during the Head section of each cycle: seven in the first cycle, five in the second, three in the third, two in the fourth, and finally one in the last cycle.
