Form
First Act - Waki Enters:
Netori Okitsuzumi → Kaikō → Nanori → Shidai → Michiyuki → Tsukizerifu
Synopsis
The priest and the two attendants are ready to leave for the capital.
Text
Poetry in regular rhythm.
Music
During the short introductory instrumental segment, the nohkan part is non-congruent with the percussion in flexible setting. The percussion parts switch to strict setting at the end of the segment and remain so during the chant sung by the waki and two wakizure. The chant is sung in tsuyogin style set in hiranori.
Visuals
The waki standing at Square 5 faces the two wakizure standing at Squares 2 and 3.

The song follows a traditional ageuta style, beginning with two lines sung by the waki and wakizure. This is however followed by an uchikiri pattern, enhanced by the nohkan playing the special takane hishigi pattern, which incorporates a high hishigi note to evoke the imagery of traveling peacefully across the ocean. In the second half, unlike in other ageuta, the mitsuji pattern appears only once, replaced by a continuous tsuzuke pattern instead. Typically, there would be a contrasting uchikiri pattern, but here it maintains its energetic character, creating an illusion of movement through time and space that is fitting for a travelling song.

The tori at the beginning, with its truncated first line, further enhances this sense of movement. As the waki performs a series of kata culminating in a tsukiashi—rising onto the toes and striking the ground with the heels—it symbolizes the journey and arrival at Takasago. The repetitive last line complements this theme of arrival, both poetically and musically, reinforcing the overall narrative of travel and destination.
