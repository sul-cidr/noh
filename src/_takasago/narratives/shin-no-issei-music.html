Synopsis
Entrance of the Suminoe who appears as an old man, and Takasago, who appears as an old woman.
Text
n/a
Music
Entrance music with a non-congruent nohkan part, and a percussion part in strict setting.
Visuals
After an extended musical introduction, the curtain raises, and the shite and the shitezure enter the stage. They stop at the 3rd and 1st Pines, respectively, where they face each other. The shite, representing Suminoe, appears as an old man, while the tsure, representing Takasago, appears as an old woman. The shite carries a rake, and the tsure carries a broom, symbolizing their enduring love and long life together.

This is the first shōdan of the Shite Enters dan, the entrance piece that brings the shite and shite-zure onto the stage; it is purposefully cast in contrasting music to introduce especially mysterious figures.

The Shin-no issei—a high-ranked form of Issei—comprises five parts:
Kakari (start),
Nagashi,
Yatsugashira,
Makuage-no-dan, and
Shizume-gashira.

In Kakari, the tempo contrasts sharply with the waki’s preceding Shidai and Michiyuki, unfolding very slowly and calmly to convey the solemnity of a First Category play and to suit the aged man and woman (shite and shitezure).

Nagashi and yatsugashira together form an extended variant of the original second part, koshi-no-dan, which is played exclusively by the two drums; as multiple koshi-no-dan variants are tied to specific plays, the choice here foreshadows the characters to come. In this version, Nagashi features the otsuzumi’s special sequence of strong strokes (measures 10–11), while yatsugashira highlights forceful kashira strokes by both otsuzumi and kotsuzumi (measures 16–20). Although “eight” (yatsu) is in the name, listeners may just hear many of such strokes; the label’s ceremonial weight matters more than a literal count.

Notably, Nagashi–Yatsugashira appears as a kogaki (“added name”) on programs, signaling a high-ranked treatment. During Makuage-no-dan (measures 22–28), the curtain rises and the shite’s party enters: unlike the waki, who always precedes his wakizure, the tsure appears first and walks ahead of the shite; the Shin-no issei serves as their entrance music as the tsure stops at the first pine and faces the shite at the third.

Finally, shizume-gashira (measures 29–30) emerges from a further ritard, culminating in measure 30 where the otsuzumi strikes strong kashira on beats 1, 3, and 5, matched by the shite and shitezure with three small steps—left, right, left. This auspicious odd-number motif returns at the play’s close, when the shite stamps left, right, left to finish.
