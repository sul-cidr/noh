Synopsis
Entrance of the Suminoe who appears as an old man, and Takasago, who appears as an old woman.
Text
n/a
Music
Entrance music with a non-congruent nohkan part, and a percussion part in strict setting.
Visuals
After an extended musical introduction, the curtain raises, and the shite and the shitezure enter the stage. They stop at the 3rd and 1st Pines, respectively, where they face each other. The shite, representing Suminoe, appears as an old man, while the tsure, representing Takasago, appears as an old woman. The shite carries a rake, and the tsure carries a broom, symbolizing their enduring love and long life together.

This is the beginning of the Shite Enters dan, the entrance music that brings the shite and shite-zure onto the stage. Compared with the preceding Waki Enters dan, the overall tempo is very slow. It conveys the solemnity of a First Category play and befits the characters of the shite and shitezure, an aged man and woman.

The Shin-no issei Music—a high-ranked form of Issei Music—comprises five parts:
Kakari (start),
Nagashi,
Yatsugashira,
Makuage-no-dan, and
Shizume-gashira.

Nagashi-Yatsugashira

Nagashi and Yatsugashira are always performed together in that order, forming an extended variant of the original second part, Koshi-no-dan. These sections are played exclusively by the two drums—the ōtsuzumi and kotsuzumi—with the nohkan silent to spotlight the percussion. Nagashi features an ōtsuzumi’s sequence of strong strokes (measures 10–11), while Yatsugashira highlights forceful kashira strokes by both drums (measures 16–20). Although “eight” (yatsu) appears in the name, the audience may not be able to distinguish that number since more than eight kashira are articulated; the label’s ceremonial and auspicious sense matters more than a literal count. Notably, Nagashi–Yatsugashira appears as a kogaki (“added name”) on programs, signaling a high-ranked treatment.

Makuage-no-dan

The word makuage means 'open the curtain'. During Makuage-no-dan (measures 22–28), the curtain rises and the shite’s party enters: unlike the waki, who always precedes his wakizure, the shitezure< appears first and walks ahead of the shite. The flow of the music matches the steps of the actors and then slows down to prepare for the closing section.

Shizume-gashira

As Shizume-gashira (measures 29–30) emerges from the slow-down, it culminates in measure 30 where the ōtsuzumi strikes strong kashira on beats 1, 3, and 5, matched by the shite and shitezure with three small steps—left, right, left. This auspicious odd-number motif returns at the play’s close, when the shite stamps left, right, left to finish. The kotsuzumi player closes this section with two high-pitched strokes (beats 6-and-a-half and 7 in measure 31). These strokes create a stillness that set the stage for the aged man and woman as they will express their poetic emotion through chanting.
