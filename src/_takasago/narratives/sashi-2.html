Kuri → Sashi 2 → Kuse
Synopsis
The old man and the jiutai discuss the pine in Takasago and other pines, noting that compared to seasonal changes, Takasago remains constant throughout the seasons. It is said that pines bloom just ten times in a thousand years, and keep inspiring our hearts.
Text
Arhythmic poetry.
Music
Sashinori chant of the shite and jiutai sung in tsuyogin style. It is supported by a non-congruent nohkan part, and a percussion part in flexible setting.
Visuals
The shite kneels on Square 9, facing front and holding the fan. For the final line of text, he slides left to face the waki.

• For the final line of text, shite slides left to face the waki as the jiutai sings 'shikishima no kage ni yoru tokaya,' a line that resonates with the themes of Japanese poetry and national identity
• Shikishima (敷島): This is an ancient poetic name for Japan, meaning "Blessed Isles". In the context of the play, it can also refer to "Shikishima no michi" or "the way of the Blessed Isles," which is a term for poetry itself.
• no Kage ni yoru (の影に寄る): This translates to "to draw nigh/approach the shadow of".
Therefore, a possible interpretation in the play is that "all living things, they say, draw nigh to the Blessed Isles," where "Blessed Isles" represents poetry or Japan itself.
It signifies the deep connection between Japanese culture, especially poetry, and the very essence of the nation, personified by the evergreen pine trees in the play. The Noh play Takasago is particularly known for its celebration of longevity, harmonious relationships, and the enduring nature of Japanese poetry and spirit.
