Ai-kyōgen
Synopsis
A local villager retells the legend of the paired pines to the priest and his attendants, enriching the narrative for the audience.
Text
Prose.
Music
A kyōgen style of recitation alternating with kotoba phrases of the waki and wakizure. Close to the end of the exchange, the nohkan player joins in.

TBD - here's what we wrote in Hasitomi:
The Ai-kyōgen interlude in Hashitomi has a typically simple intermedia character. The kyōgen actor converses with the waki with very little movement and mostly without music.

In preparation for the Ai-kyōgen, the percussionists fold their chairs, as all musicians kneel in a straight line facing sideways to indicate that although they are on stage they are not involved.

However, half way through the interlude, when the kyōgen turns back to speak with the waki, the drummers return to sitting on the chairs. It is the tradition of the Morita school of nohkan playing to layer the final section of the Ai-kyōgen, with a melodic pattern called shimo no takane. It is a non-congruent melody that includes few original patterns but also others akin to mi roku no ge, takane hane, and kuse-no-ryo. The end of the shimo no takane coincides more of less with the end of the dialogue between the kyōgen and the waki. Having memorized the text, the nohkan player positions his patterns accordingly. The melody also serves as a signal to the actors in the backstage that the second act is about to begin.
