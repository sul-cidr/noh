Form
First Act - Dialogue:
Mondō → Kakeai → Ageuta 2 → Dialogue
Synopsis
The text celebrates 'a sovereign whose blessings bring prosperity to all. In such times, the paired pines also rejoice.
Text
Poetic text in regular rhythm.
Music
This congruent chant by the jiutai is set in hiranori and sung in tsuyogin style. It is supported by a non-congruent nohkan part, and a percussion part in strict rhythmic setting.
Visuals
While the jiutai sings, the three actors reposition themselves. The shitezure moves to Square 7 and kneels, while the waki sits at Square 5. Meanwhile, the shite performs two Forward Points followed by Open-retreats, with the first one on the dominant Square 4, the next one on the weaker Square 1.

This shōdan centers on the reign, one of the principal themes of the play. More specifically, the text focuses on peace and the eternity of the reign, evoking a “calm wind” that softly blows over the four seas and the pine trees. Ageuta 2—together with the final parts of the Rongi 2—has been one of the most emblematic chants for celebratory occasions since the 17th century, including the Edo shogunate’s New Year rituals. It is still performed by all Noh schools as the first song of the New Year.

Because celebration is traditionally associated with directness, this Ageuta is sung at the fastest tempo among the chants. To avoid slowing the pace, it omits both the repetition of the first line and the insertion of an uchikiri at the outset. Although the final line is repeated for emphasis, the opening is exceptional in its unbroken forward momentum.

The stage movment helps deliniate the text. After the shite proceeds to Square 4, during the line aioi no matsu (“paired pine tree”), he performs an Open-retreat kata to indicate and contemplate the paired pines. He then turns left, moves to Square 1 and then 2, and executes another Open-retreat that this time throws a strong spotlight on kimi no megumi (“the sovereign’s blessing”), sung twice as the final line is repeated. In this fast section, two focal points stand out: the first Forward Point and Open-retreat signal his attention to the paired pines; the second set highlights the auspicious sovereign.

As is typical in the second half of an Ageuta, the nohkan enters with the naka no takane pattern. Together with the text, this heightens the emotional character of the segment and marks the customary shift from presenting new information to amplifying affect.

Besides the brief jitori in the shidai, this shōdan marks the first substantial entrance of the jiutai. In fact, Ageuta 2 is the first shōdan they carry on their own, singing at full strength.
