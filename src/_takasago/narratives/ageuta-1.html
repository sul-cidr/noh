Form
First Act - Shite Enters:
Shin no Issei Music → Shin no Issei Chant → Sashi 1 → Sageuta → Ageuta 1
Synopsis
In this scene, the focus shifts from inner reflection to observing the external environment, as the two characters describe their surroundings, particularly Takasago and its falling leaves, which symbolize the passage of time. This prompts them to ponder the question, “How much longer will we live?” The word for living, 'iki,' also evokes the Iki pine grove in Kyushu, another site celebrated in Japanese poetry.
Text
Poetic text in regular rhythm.
Music
This congruent chant sung together by shite and shitezure is set in hiranori. It is accompanied by a non-congruent nohkan part, and a percussion part in strict setting.
Visuals
Facing each other while standing on Squares 1 and 9, the old couple acknowledges the Takasago tree. They briefly turn to face the front, recognizing its old age, before turning to face each other again. The old man then walks to Square 8, while the old woman walks to Square 1, hands her broom to the stage attendant and moves to Square 2 facing front.

The celebratory nature of this performance of Takasago is particularly evident in this Ageuta. The first uchikiri (the standard musical segment that occurs between repeated lines of text) typically consists of a single 8-beat phrase, but in this special rendition, it is extended to two phrases with long, slow notes from the nohkan. Another interesting intermedia moment happens when the two characters sing the line, “How long will our lives continue?” They end with a dramatic slowdown, leading to another uchikiri and a soulful return of the nohkan, which emphasizes the question and offers a moment for reflection. The nohkan continues to play and give emphasis as they discuss the Live Pine of Iki, a celebrated subject of poetry. Additionally, when the shitzure sings alone, they use a higher pitch, creating a contrast that enhances the celebratory richness and symbolizes the interplay of Yin and Yang so important in Takasago.
