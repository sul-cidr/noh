First Act - Waki Enters:
Nanoribue → Nanori → Kakaru-1
Synopsis
The monk introduces himself, saying he is coming from Unrin-in Temple in Murasakino. He explains that he is about to perform a memorial service for the flowers that he has been offering throughout the summer.
Text
Prose.
Music
Unaccompanied vocal part of the waki delivered in kotoba.
Visuals
To deliver his monologue, the waki stands at Square 1.

The monk introduces himself and explains his intentions in the simplest way, by speaking unaccompanied. The speech is tranquil, steady, and performed almost without movement while the actor remains on Square 1. Although the overall texture remains simple, the inclusion of the narrative that answers the 'who,' 'why,' 'what' and 'where' about the monk, gives this shōdan a higher Intermedia Index than the preceding Nanoribue.

The monk concludes the Nanori by bringing his hands together, holding a Buddhist rosary in the right hand. While the origin of this gesture is believed to be related to prayer, it has become a standard closing gesture also used by secular characters such as the Emperor’s envoy (right arm)in Kokaji.
