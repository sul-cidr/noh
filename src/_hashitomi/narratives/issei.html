Form
Second Act - Shite Re-enters:
Issei → Sageuta → Ageuta-2
Synopsis
The recitation of the monk that started earlier is carried on now by the voice from the inside of the dwelling.
Text
Arhythmic poetry.
Music
The entrance Issei-music occupies the Issei’s first three of twelve honji. During the first one the rhythmic setting of percussion switches from flexible to strict. The process is perceived through the changing kakegoe when the first two extended calls of the ōtsuzumi player, which are reminiscent of the flexible setting, are answered by the kotsuzumi playerwith shorter calls, typical of the strict setting. This switch leads to the non-congruent nohkan’s mi roku no ge pattern, which in turn leads to the chanted exchange between the shite and the jiutai. The einori chant is delivered in yowagin style.
Visuals
After completing the previous module, the waki turns and goes back to his position on Square 5 and kneels. Meanwhile, the kyōgen discreetly leaves the stage, passing behind the musicians and through the door in the back stage left. Shortly after, as the voice emerges from the inside of the tsukurimono, the waki turns toward it.

The Issei-chant, introduces a counterpoint of two intertwined poems that are recited by two actors passing lines back and forth. The process starts in the preceding Kakaru, where the waki sings the first two lines of the first poem. Then, the shite opens the Issei-chant by singing the poem’s third line, but rather than finishing it, he introduces the first two lines of a second poem. It is the jiutai who completes the second poem with its last line, before singing the last two lines of the first. The process is summarized in this figure:

Sharing a poetic line (composed from two poems) between three different 'actors' signals an important moment in the overall structure. It turns away from fact-based story-telling to more abstract aesthetics of poetry, music and eventually, dance.

It is noteworthy that although the waki’s participation in the singing is over at this point in the play, he is still an active player. For instance, the moment the shite starts chanting from inside the tsukurimono the waki slides on his knee to face it. By showing that he can hear the voice he adds to its materiality even though it is produced by an invisible source. Alternatively, it could indicate that everything continues to happen in the mind of the monk.
